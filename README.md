 How do I interpret user needs and implement them into 
a program? How does creating user stories help with this?

Honestly before this course I thought understanding user 
needs just meant asking someone what they want and building 
it. That turned out to be way too simple. What I learned is 
that people do not always know how to describe what they 
actually need. They describe what they think the solution 
should look like instead of the real problem they are trying 
to solve. So the job of a developer or PM is to dig deeper 
and figure out the real need underneath the request.

User stories helped me do that in a way that actually made 
sense. Writing it as as a traveler I want to filter by budget 
so I can find trips I can afford sounds simple but it forces 
you to think about who you are building for and why. There 
were moments in this project where the team disagreed on how 
a feature should work and going back to the user story settled 
it. The story reminded us we were building for the user not 
for ourselves.

 How do I approach developing programs? What Agile 
processes do I hope to incorporate into my future 
development work?

My approach now is to start small and build up. Before this 
course I would have tried to plan everything out perfectly 
before writing a single line of code. Now I know that does 
not work because requirements change and you learn things 
as you go that you could not have known at the start.

The Agile processes I want to keep using are Sprint Planning 
because starting a sprint without a clear goal is a recipe 
for chaos, Daily Scrums because catching a problem on day 
two is so much better than finding out on day fourteen, and 
Retrospectives because I genuinely found those conversations 
useful. It felt good to reflect on what went wrong without 
it being a blame session. I also want to keep using 
acceptance criteria on every user story because it removes 
so much unnecessary back and forth about whether something 
is actually finished or not.


 What does it mean to be a good team member in 
software development?

I think it means being honest even when that is 
uncomfortable. The best thing a teammate can do is say 
I am stuck or I found a problem early enough for the team 
to do something about it. The worst thing is sitting on a 
problem because you do not want to look bad and then the 
whole sprint falls apart because of it.

This course showed me that transparency is not just a Scrum 
buzzword. It is genuinely what makes a team function. I also 
think being a good team member means doing your part without 
waiting to be told twice and being willing to help someone 
else even when it is technically not your task. Software 
development is too collaborative for anyone to succeed alone.
